# SRE Overview

## Overview

Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) is a discipline that applies software engineering principles to infrastructure and operations. Originated at Google in 2003, SRE bridges the gap between development and operations teams by treating infrastructure as software — something that can be automated, measured, and improved continuously.

The core philosophy of SRE is that reliability is a product feature. Rather than treating outages as inevitable and operational work as separate from engineering, SRE embeds reliability into the software development lifecycle from day one.

SRE teams are responsible for the availability, latency, performance, and efficiency of production services. They own incident response, change management, monitoring, and capacity planning — with an explicit goal of spending at least 50% of their time on engineering work rather than manual operations.

## When To Use

- **Building new production services** — SRE principles should inform architecture decisions from the start (redundancy, scalability, observability)
- **Mature services with reliability issues** — Apply SRE practices to identify root causes and systematically improve reliability
- **Defining service level agreements** — SRE provides the framework for setting meaningful SLAs, SLOs, and SLIs
- **Reducing operational burden** — SRE's focus on automation and toil reduction helps teams scale without linearly growing headcount
- **On-call improvement** — When pages are noisy, incidents take too long to resolve, or same issues recur
- **Incident review and learning** — SRE postmortem culture emphasizes blameless analysis and systemic improvements

## When NOT To Use

- **Greenfield projects with no users** — Focus on building and validating the product first; SRE rigor matters when you have real traffic and reliability requirements
- **Completely static infrastructure** — If infrastructure rarely changes and doesn't need to scale, traditional ops may suffice
- **Prototype/MVP phase with explicit "move fast" mindset** — Accept that reliability tradeoffs are intentional during this phase
- **Teams without bandwidth for engineering work** — SRE requires investment in automation and tooling; pure firefighting mode leaves no time for it

## Core Concepts

### The SRE Manifesto

SRE's founding principle at Google: **"Ship the software. Run the software. Fix the software."** This means engineers who build a system are also responsible for operating it in production. This ownership model eliminates the "throw over the wall" dynamic between development and operations teams.

### Error Budgets

Error budgets are the central mechanism SRE uses to balance reliability and innovation. An error budget = `1 - SLO` — the amount of unreliability you're willing to accept in a given window. If your SLO is 99.9% availability, your error budget is 0.1% of downtime allowed per month.

**Why error budgets work**: When reliability is at 100%, you have a full error budget to spend on new features. When you burn through the budget (reliability degrades), you stop feature development and focus on reliability. This creates a self-regulating mechanism that prevents both over-engineering and under-investing in reliability.

### Toil

Toil is manual, repetitive, automatable work that scales linearly with service growth. If your user base doubles and your on-call burden doubles, that's toil. SRE teams aim to keep toil below 50% of their time — the rest should be engineering: building automation, improving reliability, reducing complexity.

**Examples of toil**:
- Manually restarting failed services
- Running the same deployment commands repeatedly
- Manually updating configurations across multiple systems
- Writing the same incident summary repeatedly
- Manually verifying that a deployment succeeded

**SLO-based toil elimination**: When something breaks repeatedly, SRE asks "why does this keep happening?" and fixes the root cause rather than manually mitigating symptoms each time.

### Service Level Hierarchy

```
SLA (Service Level Agreement)
├── Legal/contractual commitment to customers
├── Financial penalties for breach
└── Derived from business requirements

SLO (Service Level Objective)
├── Internal target we aim for
├── Drives error budget policy
└── Should be stricter than SLA

SLI (Service Level Indicator)
├── What we actually measure
├── Basis for SLO calculations
└── Must be measurable and unambiguous
```

### Google SRE Book Principles

The foundational SRE practices from Google's experience:

1. **Embrace risk** — Not all failures matter equally; optimize for the failures that do
2. **Strive for simplicity** — Complex systems fail in complex ways
3. **Automate first** — Manual work doesn't scale and introduces errors
4. **Measure what's important** — You can't improve what you can't measure
5. **Learn from failures** — Blameless postmortems drive systemic improvement
6. **Shared ownership** — Developers share operational responsibility for their services
7. **Use the error budget** — Let the budget guide investment decisions

## Real Production Example

### Google-Scale SRE Practices

At Google, SRE teams typically manage services with the following characteristics:

- **Availability target**: 99.9% (8.76 hours downtime/year) for most services
- **Error budget for 99.9% SLO**: 43.8 minutes/month for a 30-day window
- **Monthly SLO burn rate alert**: Triggers when 50% of budget burns in 1 hour (fast burn) or 4 hours (slow burn)
- **On-call response**: &lt;5 minutes for P1 incidents, &lt;30 minutes for P2

### SRE in a Mid-Size Company

A company with 50-engineer engineering team might structure SRE as:

```
SRE Team (4 engineers)
├── Owns: Platform services, monitoring, incident response
├── Shared ownership: Core infrastructure, CI/CD
└── Advisory: Application reliability, deployment practices

Application Teams (5 teams × ~8 engineers)
├── Own: Application reliability for their domain
├── Participate: On-call rotation for shared services
└── Consume: Platform services and SRE-defined SLIs/SLOs
```

### SLO Configuration Example

```yaml
# Example SLO configuration for a payments service
service_level_objectives:
  - name: "API Availability"
    sli: "successful_requests / total_requests"
    target: 99.95%  # 21.9 minutes downtime/month
    window: 30d
    error_budget_policy:
      burn_threshold: 50%  # Alert when 50% budget burned
      burn_window: 1h
      action: "Page on-call; halt non-critical deployments"

  - name: "API Latency (p99)"
    sli: "requests with latency < 200ms / total_requests"
    target: 99%  # 7.3 hours of slow responses/month
    window: 30d
    alert_on: "p99 > 500ms for 5 minutes"

  - name: "Data Pipeline Freshness"
    sli: "records processed within SLA window"
    target: 99.9%
    window: 1d
    alert_on: "processing lag > 1 hour"
```

## Metrics / Formulas / Frameworks

### Error Budget Calculation

```
Monthly Error Budget (minutes) = (1 - SLO_target) × minutes_in_window

For 99.9% SLO over 30 days:
Error Budget = (1 - 0.999) × 43,200 minutes = 43.2 minutes
```

### Availability Percentages

| Availability | Downtime/Year | Downtime/Month | Downtime/Week |
|--------------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| 90% | 36.5 days | 3 days | 16.8 hours |
| 99% | 3.65 days | 7.2 hours | 1.68 hours |
| 99.5% | 1.83 days | 3.6 hours | 50.4 minutes |
| 99.9% | 8.76 hours | 43.8 minutes | 10.1 minutes |
| 99.95% | 4.38 hours | 21.9 minutes | 5.04 minutes |
| 99.99% | 52.6 minutes | 4.38 minutes | 1.01 minutes |

### Error Budget Burn Rate

```
Burn Rate = Actual Error Rate / Permitted Error Rate

For 99.9% SLO (0.001 permitted error rate):
- If actual error rate is 0.002 (0.2%), burn rate = 2.0
- At burn rate 2.0, budget depletes 2× faster than normal
- Budget exhaustion time = Error Budget / (Burn Rate × Baseline Error Rate)
```

### Toil Formula

```
Toil Percentage = (Time spent on toil / Total time) × 100

SRE target: Toil < 50% of engineering time
Warning threshold: Toil > 30% triggers investigation
```

### MTBF and MTTR

```
Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)

Example:
- MTBF = 720 hours (30 days mean time between failures)
- MTTR = 1 hour (average time to repair)
- Availability = 720 / 721 = 99.86%
```

## Monitoring / Alerting Examples

### SLO-Based Alerting

```yaml
# Prometheus alert rule for SLO error budget burn
groups:
  - name: slo-error-budget
    interval: 30s
    rules:
      # Fast burn alert (50% budget in 1 hour)
      - alert: SLOErrorBudgetFastBurn
        expr: |
          (
            1 - (
              sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status!~"5.."}[1h]))
              /
              sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api"}[1h]))
            )
          ) / (1 - 0.999) > 50
        for: 0m
        labels:
          severity: critical
          slo: api-availability
        annotations:
          summary: "API SLO error budget burning fast"
          description: "50% of error budget burned in 1 hour (burn rate: {{ $value }}x)"
          runbook_url: "https://runbooks.example.com/slo-budget-fast-burn"

      # Slow burn alert (50% budget in 4 hours)
      - alert: SLOErrorBudgetSlowBurn
        expr: |
          (
            1 - (
              sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status!~"5.."}[4h]))
              /
              sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api"}[4h]))
            )
          ) / (1 - 0.999) > 50
        for: 0m
        labels:
          severity: warning
          slo: api-availability
        annotations:
          summary: "API SLO error budget burning slowly"
          description: "50% of error budget burned in 4 hours"
```

### Golden Signals Alerting

```yaml
groups:
  - name: golden-signals
    rules:
      # Latency - p99 > 1s
      - alert: APIHighLatency
        expr: |
          histogram_quantile(0.99, 
            rate(http_request_duration_seconds_bucket{job="api"}[5m])
          ) > 1
        for: 5m
        labels:
          severity: warning
          signal: latency
        annotations:
          summary: "API p99 latency above 1 second"
          description: "p99 latency is {{ $value | humanizeDuration }}"

      # Traffic - sudden drop
      - alert: APITrafficDrop
        expr: |
          rate(http_requests_total{job="api"}[5m]) < 0.5 * 
          avg_over_time(rate(http_requests_total{job="api"}[5m])[1h])
        for: 5m
        labels:
          severity: warning
          signal: traffic
        annotations:
          summary: "API traffic dropped by more than 50%"

      # Errors - 5xx rate > 1%
      - alert: APIHighErrorRate
        expr: |
          sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status=~"5.."}[5m])) 
          / 
          sum(rate(http_requests_total{job="api"}[5m])) > 0.01
        for: 2m
        labels:
          severity: critical
          signal: errors
        annotations:
          summary: "API 5xx error rate above 1%"

      # Saturation - CPU > 80%
      - alert: APISaturation
        expr: |
          rate(process_cpu_seconds_total{job="api"}[5m]) * 100 > 80
        for: 10m
        labels:
          severity: warning
          signal: saturation
        annotations:
          summary: "API service CPU above 80%"
```

## Security

### SRE Security Responsibilities

SRE teams handle operational security aspects:

1. **Secret management** — Ensuring credentials aren't exposed in logs, configs, or code
2. **Access control** — Managing production access, break-glass procedures
3. **Security patching** — Rapid deployment of security patches (SRE owns the pipeline)
4. **Incident response** — Handling security incidents with the same rigor as reliability incidents
5. **Monitoring for threats** — Anomaly detection, brute force detection, unusual access patterns

## Cost Optimization

### SRE Cost Responsibilities

SRE teams influence infrastructure costs through:

1. **Right-sizing** — Matching capacity to actual demand (not peak + 2x)
2. **Autoscaling** — Only paying for what you use
3. **Resource efficiency** — Reducing waste from misconfigured resources
4. **SLO optimization** — Spending engineering time where it matters most

### Cost vs Reliability Balance

```
Cost Optimization Opportunities by SLO Level:

99.9% SLO (43.8 min/month downtime):
- Minimal overprovisioning needed
- Standard HA architecture sufficient
- Cost: ~$X

99.99% SLO (4.38 min/month downtime):
- Multi-AZ deployment required
- Hot standby capacity
- Additional monitoring and alerting
- Cost: ~3-5X of 99.9%

99.999% SLO (26 seconds/month downtime):
- Active-active multi-region
- Extensive redundancy
- Very high operational overhead
- Cost: ~10-20X of 99.9%
```

### Rightsizing Example

```bash
# Analyze actual CPU utilization vs provisioned
# PromQL: actual CPU usage

/ 
avg(container_spec_cpu_quota{container="api"} / container_spec_cpu_period{container="api"})

# If utilization < 30%, consider smaller instance
# If utilization > 80%, consider larger instance or scaling
```

## Troubleshooting / Common Mistakes

### Common SRE Mistakes

| Mistake | Impact | Solution |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Setting SLOs too tight | Error budget burns quickly, constant firefighting | Align SLOs with user experience, not theoretical perfection |
| No error budget policy | No clear trigger for reliability work | Define burn rate alerts and action thresholds |
| Alerting on everything | Alert fatigue, ignored pages | Only alert on SLO-impacting events |
| Manual incident response | Slow resolution, inconsistent outcomes | Document runbooks, automate detection |
| Not tracking toil | Team burns out, no time for engineering | Regular toil reviews, automation backlog |
| Skipping postmortems | Same incidents recur | Blameless culture, action item tracking |

### SLO Troubleshooting

**Symptom**: Error budget depleting faster than expected

**Diagnosis steps**:
```bash
# 1. Check if it's a spike or sustained issue
rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status=~"5.."}[5m])
# vs
rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status=~"5.."}[1h])

# 2. Check which endpoints are failing
sum by (endpoint) (rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status=~"5.."}[5m]))

# 3. Check if specific region/instance is failing
sum by (region, instance) (rate(http_requests_total{job="api",status=~"5.."}[5m]))

# 4. Check for deployment correlation
# Look at deployment timestamps in last 24 hours
kubectl rollout history deployment/api -n production
```

### Incident Anti-Patterns

```yaml
Anti-patterns to avoid:

1. "It's just a warning" - Pages that don't require action
2. "I'll fix it in the morning" - Unaddressed production issues overnight
3. "Let me check if it's real" - Unverified escalations
4. "We've always done it this way" - Ritual alerting without purpose
5. "Just restart it" - Symptom treatment without root cause analysis
```

## Interview Questions

### Q1: What is the difference between SRE and DevOps?

**A**: There's significant overlap, but key distinctions:

- **DevOps** is a cultural/philosophical movement focused on breaking down silos between development and operations, improving collaboration, and automating the software delivery process.
- **SRE** is a specific discipline that applies software engineering principles to operations. SRE was born at Google as a formal implementation of many DevOps principles.

Both aim for faster delivery with high reliability. SRE provides specific practices (error budgets, SLOs, toil tracking) and measurable targets (50% engineering time, &lt;50% toil).

### Q2: How do you decide what SLO to set for a new service?

**A**: SLOs should be set based on:

1. **User expectations**: What do users experience as "working"?
2. **Business requirements**: What's the cost of downtime vs cost of preventing it?
3. **Historical performance**: What has the service historically achieved?
4. **Competitive benchmarks**: What do similar services offer?

Process:
- Measure current performance for 2-4 weeks (SLIs)
- Set initial SLO slightly above current performance
- Tighten SLO as reliability improves
- Never set SLO tighter than what users can perceive as broken

### Q3: What would you do if an SLO's error budget is depleting faster than expected?

**A**:

1. **Verify the data** — Check if metrics are accurate, not due to measurement issues
2. **Identify the cause** — Is it a deployment, infrastructure issue, or traffic spike?
3. **Mitigate immediately** — Rollback if deployment-related, scale if load-related
4. **Communicate** — Alert stakeholders, update status page
5. **Root cause analysis** — Find and fix the underlying issue
6. **Prevent recurrence** — Add monitoring, fix systemic issues
7. **Review SLO** — Is it realistic? Should it be adjusted?

### Q4: How do you handle the tension between feature velocity and reliability?

**A**: Error budgets provide a principled way to manage this tension:

- When error budget is healthy: invest more in features
- When error budget is depleting: pause features, invest in reliability
- The budget creates a self-regulating mechanism

Key practices:
- Track error budget burn rate in real-time
- Have clear policies: "When budget < 50%, no new deployments"
- Automate reliability work so it doesn't compete with features
- Focus engineering time on highest-impact improvements

### Q5: What makes a good postmortem?

**A**: A good postmortem:

1. **Blameless** — Focus on systems and processes, not individuals
2. **Accurate** — Timeline is verified, not guessed
3. **Complete** — Covers detection, impact, mitigation, and root cause
4. **Actionable** — Every problem has a specific fix or investigation
5. **Tracked** — Action items have owners and deadlines
6. **Followed up** — Verify actions actually prevented recurrence

The goal is learning, not punishment. "What should we do better next time?" not "Who messed up?"
